1. Law of Large Numbers

Consider a sequence of $n$ i.i.d. random variables $X_1, ..., X_n$, each with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. Then, the Law of Large Numbers states that $$ S_n = \frac{X_1 + X_2 + ... + X_n}{n}\xrightarrow{n\to\infty} \mu.$$

In order to visualize the LLN, we will consider sequences of indipendent dice rolls, each with mean $\mu = 3.5$ and variance $\sigma^2 = \frac{35}{12} \approx 2.9167$.

The function np.random.randint(a, b, size = n) can be used to generate a sequence of size $n$ of random integers between $a$ and $b-1$ (included), drawn from the discrete uniform distribution.

2. Central Limit Theorem

Consider a sequence of $n$ i.i.d. random variables $X_1, ..., X_n$, each with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$. Define $$ S_n = \frac{X_1 + X_2 + ... + X_n}{n}$$ then, the Central Limit Theorem states that, as $n\to\infty$, $$Z_n \stackrel{\rm def}{=} \sqrt{n}\frac{S_n - \mu}{\sigma}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

4. LLN and matrices

Consider a matrix $X\in\mathbb{R}^{n\times d}$ having i.i.d. standard Gaussian elements $X_{ij}$, and the correspondent sample covariance matrix $\Sigma = \frac{1}{n}X^TX$. The Law of Large Numbers, applied to each element of $\Sigma$, implies, as $n\to\infty$, $$\Sigma_{ii} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k = 1}^n X_{ki}^2\to 1$$ and $$ \Sigma_{ij} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{k = 1}^n X_{ki}X_{kj}\to 0, \text{ for }i\neq j.$$

From this, one might conclude that $\Sigma \to\mathbb{I}_d$ as $n,d\to\infty$. However will show that the situation is more complex when we consider the matrix as a whole.
